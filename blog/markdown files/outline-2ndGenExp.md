# Outline: An observation as a second generation immigrant

an immigrant experience for me at least is defined by a differential on the way my parents think community works (coming from a culture that highly valued community) and the individualistic nature of America. I wasnt taught the tools I needed to live here. my parents assumed i would have a culture of community, and the lack left me somewhat alone.

## my indian american experience, in brief
My parents are from India; for most of my life that meant mosquitoes and unbearable heat every summer vacation. As a child, this felt like a terrible punishment.

Now with bit more wisdom, I feel immense gratitude at the opportunity.

It  meant having the opportunity to learn one of the oldest surviving languages in the world.

I got to experience culture and architecture thousands of years old.

But I felt at arm's length, as if I were an outsider, rather than part of it.

##
To me, being an Indian-American is a mutually exclusive experience from being an Indian. Being one, I might never have the other.

And likewise, my parents will never be Indian-American.

Many children of immigrants will resonate: parents have a fundementally different understanding of the world than their children do.

Me and my parents both have different assumptions of the world. Not in terms of values or knowledge, but something more simple: they think the world is small, and I think it is big.

Let me explain:

My parent's lived experience with education had been pretty consistent; the whole time, you saw the same people. Years and years of history sat behind every name. Every relationship had weight. In fact, I imagine it was hard to think of a relationship that didn't have weight; everyone that they knew, they knew well. A small, tightly knit village.

Contrast that experience with my own: I made brand new relationships every year, and every classroom was filled with different people. Every few years, we were shuffled to a new school to with a new set of faces.

I had a radically different understanding of what *community* meant. To me, they were transient, more like venn diagrams that I freely floated through. I imagine that for my parents, community must feel so much more solid and static.

Crucially, both me and my parents *coudn't even comprehend something __could be different between us__*.

I had to learn for myself what it meant to be a kid in America. Without a shared understanding of that experience with my parents, it was impossible to communicate mundane necessities:
- I needed to be driven *everywhere*. My school didn't offer busses for afterschool clubs, and so I felt like I was unfairly impacting my parents time. Ditto for going to my friend's houses.
- All the dangers of protecting your kid was known to my mom, but none of the usual solutions were available. There were no grandparents to drop off the kids for a weekend. Because the suburban neighborhood in America was more unfamiliar to my parents, I never learned that it was normal to talk to your neighbors.
