# Outline: An observation as a second generation immigrant

an immigrant experience for me at least is defined by a differential on the way my parents think community works (coming from a culture that highly valued community) and the individualistic nature of America. I wasnt taught the tools I needed to live here. my parents assumed i would have a culture of community, and the lack left me somewhat alone.

## my indian american experience, in brief  
My parents are from India; for most of my life that meant mosquitoes and unbearable heat every summer vacation. As a child, this felt like a terrible punishment.

It also meant having the opportunity to learn one of the oldest surviving languages in the world. Now with bit more wisdom, I feel immense gratitude at the opportunity.

I got to experience culture and architecture thousands of years old. But I felt kept at arm's length, as if it were a culture that I could only observe, rather than live. 

## 
I learned firsthand how being an Indian-American is a distinct experience from being an Indian. Being one, I might never have the other. 

And likewise, my parents will never be Indian-American. 

##
I have had a rich and fulfilling experience as an Indian American. I appreciate my unique perspective compared to my parents. 

Yesterday I realized there was yet another difference between my parents and I that had lay in wait for all these years, silently leading to miscommunications and missed opportunities.

My parents have a fundementally different understanding of the world than I do. Not in terms of values or knowledge, but we both have different assumptions of the world. In short, they think the world is small: I think it is big.

My parent's experience with education had been pretty consistent; the whole time, you tend to see the same people. Years and years of built up relationships. Their world was one with deep history; every relationship had weight. In fact, it was hard to think of a relationship that didn't have weight; everyone that they knew, they knew well.

As I grew up through the American educational system, I saw brand new people every year. Every classroom was filled with different people, and every few years, I was shuffled to a new school to learn a new set of faces.

I am not saying that in this environment I couldn't have built the same weighty relationships my parents did in India. However, *they didn't even comprehend something __could be missing__*.

I had to learn for myself what it meant to be a kid in America. Without a shared understanding of that experience with my parents, it was impossible to communicate mundane necessities: 
- I needed to be driven *everywhere*. My school didn't offer busses for afterschool clubs, and so I felt like I was unfairly impacting my parents time. Ditto for going to my friend's houses.
- All the dangers of protecting your kid was known to my mom, but none of the usual solutions were available. There were no grandparents to drop off the kids for a weekend. Because the suburban neighborhood in America was more unfamiliar to my parents, I never learned that it was normal to talk to your neighbors.
